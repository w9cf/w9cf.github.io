I prefer to use Gaussian units rather than the SI units generally used in engineering. I find that the unnecessary additional unit of current in SI hides the physics. For example the conductivity of copper in Gaussian units is tex2html_wrap_inline$5.2 ×#times;10^17$ secondtex2html_wrap_inline$^-1$ so you know immediately that for all frequencies well below optical ones that tex2html_wrap_inline$&sigma#sigma;&Gt#gg;&omega#omega;$, and the time derivative of tex2html_wrap_inline$D$ can be ignored compared to the tex2html_wrap_inline$&sigma#sigma;E$term. In SI units neither tex2html_wrap_inline$&epsi#epsilon;$ nor tex2html_wrap_inline$&sigma#sigma;$ are in easy to understand units and you have to eliminate the ampere units before the physics becomes clear. An analog of this is the calculation of volume in gallons or bushels in the english system compared to calculating the volume in cubic meters in metric systems. For those units SI did the right thing, for electrical measurements I find SI is like using the english system. For relativistic calculations tex2html_wrap_inline$E$ and tex2html_wrap_inline$B$, and tex2html_wrap_inline$D$ and tex2html_wrap_inline$H$ are components of the same second rank tensor. Measuring them in different units like SI does is cumbersome. Gaussian (and Heaviside-Lorentz) have the additional advantage of much cleaner relativistic notation. See any advanced text on electromagnetism if you find this to be a problem.
